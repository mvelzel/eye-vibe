## How it was cracked

### 1. Recover structure without assuming #1's answer

The community first grouped ciphertext letters by their preferred position
modulo 42.  Two statistical clusters emerged.  Long windows with the same
cluster-membership pattern then exposed probable repeated plaintext; their
within-cluster distances changed by only `0` or `±1`.  That is the signature
of a one-directional drift: two compared states may both advance, neither may
advance, or only one may advance.

The lone `J` in section 10 was even more diagnostic.  The positional plots
change direction exactly there.  This observation preceded any plaintext
identification.

### 2. Use #1 as structural meta-knowledge

The two statistical clusters match #1's recovered 41-cycles exactly, including
their internal ordering.  Interleaving them gives the same 82-position wheel:

```text
p>9iEZ81q-',D[].6f$0;<jl2Q7_kdP&`BLFng"5R^X:?V@OmG/+re~oUa=3)c\h!4YSKT*N(#HMbCW%IA
```

(`~` denotes the literal-space ciphertext symbol.)  Replacing each ciphertext
symbol by its wheel coordinate turns adjacent pairs into directed distances
modulo 82.  Accumulating those distances modulo 42 collapses each ordinary
section to a monoalphabetic plaintext-coordinate stream.  Its IoC is about
0.07–0.08 rather than the raw delta stream's roughly random value.

### 3. Recover the shared plaintext wheel

The section modes expose space, and ordinary substitution solving immediately
produces English.  The first section reads as the opening of Rune XLIX.  Source
synchronization across eleven sections shows that their apparent substitution
keys are only rotations of one wheel.  Combining those rotations recovers all
letters and punctuation and reveals the alphabet above; the unused positions
are the digits in their natural order.

The section rotations are finite and explicit:

```text
0, 0, 12, 38, 24, 32, 18, 23, 2, 30, [reversal], 3, 28
```

### 4. Treat `J` as an operation, not a letter

In section 10, `J` occurs at zero-based position 90, exactly at the doubled L
in `WILL`.  It emits the repeated plaintext letter and reverses both wheel
traversals.  Before it, ciphertext and plaintext coordinates advance; after
it, both differences are taken in the opposite direction and the plaintext
coordinate map is reflected.  That produces the complete stanza, including
`WILL ASSUME A SECOND BODY`, without a language-model patch.

## Verification

The implementation checks the reversal at the sole `J` and shows that all
6,748 decoded characters use the single recovered plaintext wheel.  Tests pin every section
by SHA-256, as well as the source-identifying openings and the reversal phrase.

The original generator was lost with the author's code for practice ciphers
1–4, so this is a deterministic full decode rather than a claim to reproduce
the author's source program byte for byte.

## Transfer to the Eyes

- Statistical position bands can recover hidden cycle membership before the
  exact cycle order is known.
- Distances varying only by `0` or `±1` are evidence for asynchronous drift,
  not failed repetition matching.
- A rare symbol can be a state transition that reverses traversal; assigning
  it a plaintext frequency destroys the invariant.
- Once one practice cipher yields a group/cycle skeleton, reuse that skeleton
  as an explicitly disclosed hypothesis on related ciphertexts, then demand a
  new independent verification such as a source match.
